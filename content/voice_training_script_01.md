# Voice Training Script — Tournament Report: Third Break

*Read this naturally, like you're telling a friend at the table about your session. Don't rush. Pause where it feels natural. This is how your content will sound.*

---

Alright so we just hit the third break and I'm sitting on about 310k. This puts me at 6.2 times the starting stack, which is good and I'm feeling pretty good about my spot right now. The goal is 8x starting stack when you are on the bubble and tournament average right now is 4.5x starting. so I've got a comfortable cushion above average and we're 27 from the money.

Now I want to talk about a hand that just happened because I think it's a really interesting spot that a lot of players would get wrong.

Ok, So we're at 1k, 1500, 1500 blinds and it folds to me and I have ace jack off-suite in the hijack. At this spot in the tournament I use 3500 for my standard open here, I'm incrementing down from the deep stack sizing of 3.5bb down to 2.2bb, here we're at 2.33bigs. It folds around to the big blind, who calls. I've been watching him for a while and I have concluded that he's an aggressive player. By that I mean He's been three-betting light, and he defends his bb wide. he's the type of player who's not afraid to put you in tough spots. That matters for how I'm going to think about this hand later.

The flop comes J76, w/ two spades. Pot is 6.1bb- 9.5k

I've got top pair, best kicker on a pretty clean flop for my range. I c-bet around sixty-five percent pot — 6k. And this is where it gets interesting.

Big blind check-raises all in for his remaining twenty-five big blinds. So I'm facing 31k to call into a pot that's going to be — let me think — 9.5k in the middle, plus my 6, plus his shove of 37k. So I'm calling 31k more into a pot of about 83k. I'm getting almost three to one.

[pause]

Ok so here's how I'm thinking about this. Let's break down what hands my opponent can have here.

For value, what is he check-raising all in with on this board? Sets — pocket sevens and pocket sixes, but Pocket jacks are unlikely since he would have 3-bet that hand preflop, and I have a jack. Two pair — jack seven suited is out there. Six Seven suited is definitely in his range from the big blind. So we've got maybe four combos of sets and two or three combos of two pair. Call it six to seven combos of value hands that are crushing me.

Now what about the bluffs and semi-bluffs? This is the part that matters. He's an aggressive player on a board with two spades and a connectors. Realistically he could have any two spades here. Ace-five of spades, king-ten of spades, nine-eight of spades, ten-nine of spades. There's a ton of flush draw combos. Then you've got straight draws — ten-nine, nine-eight, eight-five, five-four suited. Some of those overlap with the spade draws. And knowing this player, he could also be doing this with just pure air — ace-high no draw, king-high with backdoor outs — just trying to take advantage of the fact that most people are going to shut down facing an all-in check-raise this close to the money.

When I count it up, I'm looking at maybe six or seven value combos and probably fifteen to twenty combos that are either draws or pure bluffs. That's close to a three-to-one ratio of bluffs to value. And I'm getting close to three-to-one on the call. The math lines up.

[pause]

Now here's the thing — a lot of players in this spot, twenty-seven from the money, they're going to fold top pair here. They're going to say, I can find a better spot. I don't want to risk my stack this close to the money. And I get that instinct. But that thinking is exactly why aggressive players keep getting away with this play. If you're folding top pair good kicker on a board that hits your range, you are over-folding. And when you over-fold, you're printing money for every aggressive player at your table.

The other thing to consider is my stack. I'm at four point two X. Even if I call and lose, I still have about twenty-six thousand — roughly one point seven X starting stack. That's short but it's playable. I'm not dead. If I win this pot, I'm up over a hundred thousand and I'm one of the bigger stacks at the table heading into the money. The risk-reward here favors the call.

[pause]

So I call. He turns over nine-eight of spades. Open-ended straight flush draw. He's got a ton of equity — actually about forty-five percent — but I'm still ahead right now. Turn is a brick — the two of diamonds. River is the king of clubs. My jacks hold and I scoop a massive pot.

But here's what I want to emphasize — even if that river was a spade and I lost, the call is still correct. You can't results-orient this. The math said call, the range analysis said call, and the pot odds said call. That's all you can control. The rest is just cards.

Twenty-seven from the money, sitting on a hundred and two thousand chips. Let's go.
